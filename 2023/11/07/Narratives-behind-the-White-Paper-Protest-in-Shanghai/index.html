Narratives behind the White Paper Protest in Shanghai

Posted on 2023-11-07

Introduction

At the end of 2022, a series of unprecedented protests against strict COVID-19 policy took
place in the People’s Republic of China. These protests are known as the White Paper Protests
or A4 Revolution. (白纸革命/A4 革命) The White Paper Protests were triggered by the 2022
Ürümqi fire and started at the Communication University of China, Nanjing (CUCN). Students
in CUCN organised spontaneously to pay tribute to victims in Ürümqi by holding White A4
papers that symbolises the censorship by the official government to prevent its people from
attaining such information. The protests quickly spread across the country and transformed
into a large civil movement to show people’s exhaustion about the brutal Zero-covid policy.
In this essay, I will use narrative analysis to show the dynamics of political mobilisation and
protest by demonstrating how sorrowful narratives related to China’s covid policy enabled
such a nationwide demonstration that is rare and almost impossible to organize under the
absolute rule of the Chinese Communist Party. This essay is divided into two parts and adopts
the approach to narrative analysis by Francesca Polletta (2006 p166-167), who suggests
identifying two things: a) the feature of narrative that allows it to achieve certain rhetorical
effects, b) the social conditions in which those rhetorical effects are likely to be politically
consequential.

The features of narratives in the White Paper Protests

Ürümqi is the capital city of Xinjiang province, the home of the Uyghur people. There was a
few cases of covid spread in Xinjiang since August 2022, the local government followed the
‘Zero-covid’ policy and adopted hostile means such as lockdowns and mandatory testing to
prevent the spreading of the epidemic. (ABC News, 2022) When the fire broke out in the
Jixiangyuan community, the fire engines could not approach the building as a crucial rescue
passageway was blocked by bollards for traffic control (Wang and Liu, 2022) and other roads
were blocked by cars that could not move because their batteries were dead from having not
been used for too long due to the lockdown. (Che and Chien, 2022) As a result, it took three
hours and a half to extinguish the fire and ten cases of death were reported, including a threeyear-old child. (Hoshur, 2022)
The fire in Ürümqi was not an isolated tragedy that relates to China’s hostile Zero-covid policy.
Two months prior to the Ürümqi fire, on the eighteenth of September, a bus in Guizhou
province that carried 47 passengers crashed on its way to a quarantine facility, resulted over
half of the passengers’ death. Under China’s Zero-covid policy, only a few positive cases of
covid could inform large-scale lockdowns and virus tests. After the accident, the government
official was ambiguous about the reason that causes the passengers to quarantine, the only
explanation was that they are “epidemic-related”. It is unclear to us whether they were tested
positive for the virus or had contact with those positive patients. Emotional comments on the
internet that expresses their outrage about the accident were removed by censors, and some
of the users’ accounts were abandoned (Wang and Dong 2022).
Earlier in the same year, serval pregnant women that were about to labour were rejected for
immediate medical care because their reports of a negative covid test exceeded four hours.
A picture of a bleeding pregnant woman sitting in front of the gate of the hospital went viral
on the internet, the baby died after she finally got to enter the hospital three hours later (BBC
News, 2022). Again, comments about the event were censored.
There are four common features in these tragedies: i) Unnecessary irreversible body damage
ii) Directly and indirectly related to the Zero-covid policy iii) Completely random and
unpredictable. Iv) Comments about these events that do not follow mainstream state media
will be censored These features echo Polltta’s (2006) proposition of qualities required for
stories to own rhetorical force, which are a) Eliciting an emotional identification with the
stories’ protagonists, b) Enable people to identify with experiences that are very far from their
own. The two conditions must be fulfilled simultaneously in order to produce sympathetic
understanding that leads to effecting changes. If only a) was fulfilled, then there is a possibility
that we may identify emotionally with a story but do not feel the necessity to do anything to
make a difference, and vice versa for the condition where only b) was fulfilled.
The shocking picture of the pregnant woman bleeding in front of the hospital is emotionally
strong enough to call for sympathy from whoever sees it while almost all citizens in China
experienced at least once of sudden lockdown due to positive covid cases that appears from
nowhere and they have all more or less suffered from the practical as well as emotional
disruption of lockdown. Thus, the narratives created a sense of “this could also happen to me
and my family, and I am even not allowed to talk about it.” Such humiliation of civil rights
evokes the people of China to go onto the streets to finish the endless miserable Zero-covid
policy by waving white A4 papers to address the elephant in the room.

Social conditions that enabled the White Paper Protests

There are three social conditions that built the foundation of White Paper Protests: i) the
appearance of the Coronavirus Omicron variant, ii) the development of effective vaccines iii)
The economic hardship as a repercussion of the Zero-covid policy.
When the epidemic first began in late 2019, the virus remained mysterious to the public. All
we knew was that it is a highly contaminating disease and 14% of the infected have server
complications including breathlessness and pneumonia. (Dehkordi et al 2020 p868) The
lockdown policy was proven to be an effective measure to control the spreading of such
devastating virus and was adopted by not only China but also Italy, Spain and the United
Kingdom. (Dehkordi et al. 2020) (Brown et al. 2021) Due to quick response, China controlled
the epidemic and its economic activities recovered within 3 months. As a result, China was
the only main economy that achieved a positive GDP growth rate of 2.2% in 2020 by
comparison of -11% in the United Kingdom, -2.8% in the United States and -4.5% in Japan.
(World Bank 2022)
However, the appearance of Omicron, a much more infectious and less dangerous variant
(McMahan, 2022) and the development of vaccines turned the covid virus into a manageable
disease for individuals. China’s strict means to eliminate the virus were becoming less effective
when facing Omicron and individuals are exhausted with frequent PCR tests and sudden
lockdowns. In other words, what people are afraid of since Omicron is not having serious
symptoms from Omicron, but the mandatory quarantine and inconvenience brought by the
Zero-covid policy itself. Economically, the service sector suffered particularly from the Zerocovid policy. On the one hand, people were going out less due to fear of having contact with
the covid; on the other hand, restaurants, hotels, and nightclubs could be forced to close for
months if there is one covid case. As a result, China’s non-manufactory business activity index
fell by 2% by December 2022 (National Development and Reform Commission, 2022). The
manufactory industry was also hit hard by the Zero-Covid policy. Toyota announced a
statement to cut production of approximately 50,000 units in 2022 due to the impact of the
lockdown in Shanghai. (Toyota, 2022) Later after the announcement, Toyota’s company
shares fall by 4.5% on the Tokyo Stock Exchange (Hoskins, 2022). Which raises political
concerns for foreign investors to expand their factories in China. As the data informs, China’s
Zero-Covid was becoming increasingly unnecessary and resulting in huge damage to socialeconomical life. But there is a ratchet effect on the Chinese government to not alter its policy
due to prior propaganda on its earlier success. As there is no sign when this miserable policy
will end, people with no choices, had to go onto the streets, waving ironic white papers to
express their dissatisfaction about the zero-covid policy and call for changes.

Conclusion

This essay analyses how woeful narratives ignited people’s fire to conduct White Paper Protest
under great risk of being arrested by the government in order to call for an end to the
miserable Zero-Covid policy. Firstly, the narratives in China reached magnificent rhetorical
effect by possessing features of unpredictable secondary disasters caused by the Zero-covid
policy but not by the coronavirus itself, for which individuals could identify emotional
connection between the protagonists’ tragedy with similarities of their own experiences.
Secondly, the fact that the virus was much more transmissible and less dangerous combined
with the development of effective vaccines gave the people good reasons to stop the policy
that brought them considerable economic burdens. To conclude, we can discover from the
case of the White Paper Protest that the power of narratives is irresistible, despite a strict
censorship system, the narratives could still gather people together on protests, it is able to
turn its greatest enemy of censorship system into protesters’ weapon as a metaphoric white
paper to against this very system
