Are impersonal structures more important than individual agency in understanding political events?

Posted on 2023-11-07

Introduction

Structure, an essential term to brought up when explaining political events which own a
feature of excludability, as addressed by William H. Sewell. Jr. (1992:2) that: “What tends to
get lost in the language of structure is the efficacy of human action – or ‘agency’.” This raises
the question of the level of importance between impersonal structures and individual agency
in understanding political events. Based on the duality structural theory by Anthony Giddens
and William H.’s modification of the theory, this essay analyses political turbulence using the
empirical case study of China’s cultural revolution to answer the question between impersonal
structure and individual agency. I argue that there is a dialectical relationship between the
former and the latter, there is no priority between the two prospects, and both need to be
addressed sufficiently in order to understand a political event such as domestic turmoil.

The Duality of structure and its application in the Cultural Revolution.

Anthony Giddens (1984:377, quoted in William H. 1992) defined structure as: “Rules and
resources, recursively implicated in the reproduction of social systems. Structure exists only
as memory traces, the organic basis of human knowledgeability, and as instantiated in action.”
What he means by this sentence is that structure must be regarded as “dual”, structure and
agency are not opposed, but acts presuppose to each other. The presupposed manner is
presented as the interaction between rules and resources. Rules, in Wittgenstein’s language,
are “generalizable procedures applied in the enactment/reproduction of social life”.
(Wittgenstein, 1984:21) The knowledge of rules makes individual agents capable of action
and such knowledge comes mainly from cultural backgrounds. While resources are the
material beings where power originates and allow it to exercise in social interactions. William’s
interpretation of the dialectical relationship between rules and resources is that: on the one
hand, resources are an instantiation of schemas, therefore inculcate and justify schemas; on
the other hand, schemas not empowered or reproduced by resources will unavoidably be
abandoned and forgotten. (William H. Sewell. Jr. 1992:13)
Gidden’s theory has perfect application in the cultural revolution, a political turmoil that lasted
for ten years that caused millions of deaths (Lucian W. Pye 1986:597), described by the
Chinese people as “Ten years of catastrophe” (十年浩劫). Here I argue that the Cultural
Revolution, an irrational political event, an event of “collective madness” (Anne F. Thurston
1984-1985), cannot be understood solely from a “structure” or “individual agency”
perspective. During this event, there is a top-down process that the duality of structure and
agency as rules and resources interacting and reinforcing each other finally led to this political
tragedy. This event first started in Mao Zedong, the supreme leader’s mind, then gradually
spread into the Chinese people’s minds and turned them into extremists and injected
murderous insanity into the society. Therefore, the Cultural Revolution can be explained by
three factors: 1) Mao’s personality, 2) Chinese traditional culture 3) People’s devotion to Mao.
(Lucian W. Pye 1986) It is the last two factors that enabled the first factor to initiate Cultural
Revolution by conducting it. As a country ruled by thousands of years of feudal monarchy
under tight control over its people, there is a tendency of the society to take seriously all that
was said and done by its leaders without doubts. Thus, any preferences of the Chinese leaders
automatically become the motives of their actions. (Lucian W. Pye 1986:601) Personally, in his
early life, Mao Zedong was educated in schools during the Qing dynasty based on Confucius
principles. (Panstov & Levine, 2013:14-15) Despite his belief in Marxism later, the Confucius
convention that promotes patriotism and the ultimate goal of becoming sainted by bringing
good virtue and prosperity to society had a great influence on him. Rather than worshipping
a transcendental god, Confucianism idolizes great emperors. We can find the influence of
Confucianism on Mao in his poem Snow – to the tune of Chin Yuan Chun (1936), in which he
compared himself with great ancient leaders such as Genghis Khan and wrote: “All are past
and gone!/For truly great men/Look to this age alone.” Mao conducted Cultural Revolution
in the request for revolutionary immortality, in other words, to accomplish the ultimate goal
set by Confucius. The Chinese traditional culture made a vast number of human resources
available to the agent Mao, while this event was in process, each poster stuck, each massacre
toward intellectuals and progressists conducted, each “little red book” (sacred text for Mao’s
personality cult) printed, had reinforced Mao’s power and in the meanwhile made Mao more
determined in his catastrophic decision. (Lucian W. Pye 1986)
What can be discovered in the event of the Cultural Revolution is the unstoppable collective
power of structure and agents. This event would be impossible to occur without the two forces
acting in census. From solely a structural perspective, we cannot explain the motive behind
Mao to make this disastrous decision that ruins the country he once fought for; from solely
an agent perspective, we cannot explain why other powerful agents in the party such as Zhou
Enlai, Deng Xiaoping and Hu Yaobang, failed to stop this tragedy from happening.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the analysis of the Cultural Revolution through the lens of the duality of
structure and agency reveals that both impersonal structures and individual agency are
equally important in understanding political events. The Cultural Revolution is a prime
example of the dialectical relationship between structure as knowledgeable rules and agents
as human resources and how they interact to create a collective madness that leads to a
catastrophic event. Mao’s personality motivated him to initiate and conduct the Cultural
Revolution, while the Chinese political conventions provided the rules that justified Mao’s
power together with the Individual Chinese agent’s devotion that contributes to it. Therefore,
to understand a political event such as the Cultural Revolution, we need to examine both the
structural and the agential aspects of the event and the interaction between the two.
Ultimately, the analysis of the Cultural Revolution highlights the importance of taking a
nuanced and comprehensive approach to the study of political events.
