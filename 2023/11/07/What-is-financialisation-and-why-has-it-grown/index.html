What is financialisation and why has it grown?

Posted on 2023-11-07

Introduction

Over the past decade, discussions over the term ‘financialization’ among scholars has
emerged dramatically, with the annually published journal articles on financialization more
than quadrupled (Web of Science 2019, quoted in Mader et al. 2020). Books and publications
about financialization have grown from a handful number since the beginning of the
millennium to more than a dozen published each year, particularly after the 2008 financial
crisis. (WorldCat, 2019, quoted in Mader et al. 2020) Debates about financialization over its
definition, features and effects are fierce and transdisciplinary (Mader, Mertans and Zwan
2020). The topic of financialization is not only limited to Economics and Political studies but
also involves sociology, anthropology, and geography studies. The essence of financialization
is intricate and heterogeneous, for instance, Malcolm Sawyer (2014) suggests that financial
development is positively related to economic development but meanwhile injects fragility
into society; while Gerald Epstein (2005) argues that there is a negative correlation between
the financial power and the society’s well-being. This essay aims to introduce financialization
from different aspects and address its importance and operational mechanism. In the essay,
firstly the definition of financialization given by representative scholars will be discussed in
terms of their width and depth; after that, we will look into the causes of financialization and
why has it grown. Finally, the effects of financialization on society will be assessed.

Conceptualisation of financialisaton

Considering the broad number of definitions given by different scholars from various
backgrounds, it is necessary to set up a standard before we investigate those definitions in
order to anchor our analysis. Philip Mader, Daniel Mertens and Natascha van der Zwan (2020)
pointed out two risks when conceptualising financialization due to its transdisciplinary nature.
The first risk is conceptual dilution: if the adoption of the term ‘financialization’ across various
disciplines requires a generic understanding of its meaning, then the concept of
financialization will lose its scientific value. In other words, its methodological value to
generalize the world and create a precise common language for scholars to discuss and serve
as a ‘data container”(Sartori 1970), otherwise, when people are discussing financialization,
each of them might mean a different thing. Another risk is solidification: if one standardized
definition becomes the norm in a particular sub-field, then a too rigid understanding of
financialization will lead to blindness of its analysis across its causes and effects on different
areas. To sum up, financialization is a term too broad to be defined as one single definition,
a successful definition of financialization should follow these principles: 1) limiting, explicitly
distinguish what falls into the category of financialization 2) mechanism-oriented, to involve
the linkage of cause and effect 3) contextual, clarifies in what context the definition is valid
(Mader, Mertans and Zwan 2020)
Natascha van der Zwan (2014) provided us with a helpful tool to organise definitional issues
and approaches, she classified three levels of analysis to the conceptualization of
financialization, they are: (a)macro-level approach, which focuses on a macroeconomicaggregate level and often engages with a state/market-dichotomy in the process of
financialization (b)meso-level approach, which emphasis on businesses and its changing
relations with financial markets. (c) micro-level, which keeps an eye on how (mostly ordinary)
individual agents are involved in the process of financialization.
“The origins of the term ‘financialization’ are obscure”, said Malcolm Sawyer (2014), “… the
current usage of the term ‘financialization’ owes much to the work of Kevin Philips.” Philips
(1994, quoted in Sawyer M. 2014:6) defined financialization as “a prolonged split between the
divergent of real and financial economies.” This is a rough and raw definition of which
elements within that definition need to be further defined. Later at the start of the millennium,
three widely accepted definitions were given by Gerald Epstein(2005), Greta Krippner(2005)
and Engelbert Stockhammer (2004). Epstein and Krippner both focused on a macro level of
analysis, Epstein defined financialization as: “ the increasing role of financial motives, financial
markets, financial actors and financial institutions in the operation of the domestic and
international economies.” While Krippner defined financialization as : “ A pattern of
accumulation in which profits accrue primarily through financial channels rather than through
trade and commodity production.”. Epstein’s definition contains a broad range of sectors that
performs financial power. Overall, it achieved an appropriate balance between conceptual
dilution and solidification by providing the object of study, but there are still flaws in it as it
does not specify the time period and geographical space it applies. (Sawyer M.2014) In
comparison, Krippner’s definition is more specific in the aspect of ‘profit-financialisaton’, his
definition can be seen as a modified version of Philip’s definition given at the beginning of
this paragraph, it classified ‘divergent of real and financial economies’ as profit attained
primarily through financial channels rather than trading of actual commodities. Differ from
Epstein and Krippner, Stockhammer’s conceptualisation of finalisation lies on a meso-level of
analysis, he defined it as: “increased activity of non-financial businesses on financial markets,
[…] measured by the corresponding income streams.” (Stockhammer, 2004) Stockhammer’s
definition provides a clear context of a business environment in which financialization occurs,
furthermore, it is also mechanism oriented as the cause (increased activity), subject(nonfinancial businesses), consequences and measurements (corresponding income steams) are
all involved. This essay suggests a flexible adoption of these definitions, considering the
transdisciplinary nature of finalisation, all the definitions mentioned above have their
emphasises and missing parts, when discussing finalisation, one should first clarify the area
and subject he is studying and then set up his approach or level of analysis for choosing the
appropriate definition to use.

Why has financialisaton grown?

Even though the origin of financialization as a term to be used remains obscure, the point
where the current wave of financialization as a process occurred is obvious. Figure I and II
illustrates the US financial assets as percentage of GDP and US financial industry profits as a
share of total US economy profits, they both showed a significant rise during the 1980s, where
the value of financial assets as percentage of GDP more than doubled from 194% to 417%
during the period and financial industry profits as a share of total economy profits for the first
time broke 20% to 30% in 1993.

The data provided us with a valuable starting point to discuss the growth of finalisation, to
answer why has financialization grown, we should firstly excavate what happened during the
1980s. In August 1981, President Ronald Reagan signed the Economic Recovery Tax Act that
began a series of economic reforms lead by neo-liberal thoughts (Steger and Roy, 2010) to
reduce government involvement in the economy by structural changes such as reduction in
both individual and corporate tax rate, the expensing of depreciable property and
government regulation. (Presidential Foundation& Institute). Similar economic reforms were
adopted by Margret Thatcher in the United Kingdom and soon spread across the world. This
suggests that financialization is positively correlated with encourage of market-led economic
mechanisms, this finding corresponds to the FESSUE (Financialisaton Economy Society and
Sustainable Development) description of financialization that the process of financialization
has been closely interwoven with deregulation of the financial system itself and the economy
more generally. (FESSUD 2011, quoted in Sawyer M, 2014) Yingyao Wang(2015), used the
empirical case study of China to illustrate the importance of institutional infrastructure in the
process of financialization. She argues that the Chinese case of financialization contains three
processes: the introduction of shareholder value by the state to manage its assets that leads
to an increase in the number of state asset management organisations and the availability of
structured investment vehicles to finance fixed asset investments. Duménil and Lévy (2001,
quoted in Epstein, 2005) see financialization, neoliberalism and globalization as three deeply
related phenomena that mark the current world economy. They argue that neoliberalism is
the ‘ideological expression of the reassertion of the power of finance” and that the return of
finance to hegemony was “accomplished in close connection with the internationalization of
capital and the globalization of markets.” (p.579) Technological innovation accelerates the
process of financialization, with the improvement in telecommunication, information could
be exchanged in an unprecedented pace. Alongside is the introduction of the computer that
enabled quantitative trading, made possible for countless transactions to occur within
seconds. There are also cultural effects contribute to financialization, as stated by
FESSUD(2011, quoted in Sawyer M, 2014) that: “Consumption has often been sustained by
the extension of credit, not least through the use of capital gains in housing as collateral. […]
Financialization is associated with a particular culture which is to be interpreted broadly.”
Before I make a conclusion of this paragraph, I would like to bring up Malcolm Sawyer(2014)’s
point that the term ‘financialization’ is not limited to a specific period or place. The analysis
of this essay is focused on the post-80s financialization led by the United State because its
degree is unprecedented, and its influence is ever-lasting. The causes of post-80s
financialization can be included as 1) Structural change led by neo-liberal principles made by
major economic entities such as The United States, The United Kingdom, and The People’s
Republic of China. 2) Technological innovation that improves the efficiency of financial
transactions vastly. 3) Change in the social environment that made individuals to be more
engaged in financial activities. Financialization is the process of these three factors consistently
interacting and enhancing each other that shapes society.

The effects of financialization

The impact of financialization is another controversial topic, with scholars including Epstein,
Godechot and Andreas Nölke arguing that financialization is fundamentally detrimental to
our society, which promotes instability and inequality. Epstein (2005:5) remarked such
influence in his book Introduction: Financialization and the World Economy that: “ […] that
some effects of financialization – in concert with neoliberalism and globalization – have been
highly detrimental to significant numbers of people around the globe.” In terms of inequality,
Godechot(2020) addressed inequality fueled by financialization in micro-level between
individuals, he argues that financialization has created “a niche with very high wages”. Within
those niches, financiers have a “hold up power” over their firms, such power increases the
finance wage premium gap and produces a sharp increase in wage inequality. Bruno Bonizzi,
Annina Kaltenbrunner and Jeff Powell (2022) analyzed inequality between the countries. They
pointed out a ‘subordinate’ state of which developing countries are integrated into the global
economy, the US dollar market-based finance and the internationalization of productive
capital, altered the geographic transfer of value from subordinate regions to superordinate
ones. The hierarchical nature is embedded in global monetary and production relations. In
terms of instability, figure 3
depicts the frequency of banking crisis by year. There is a period
during the 1940s to 1970s, under the Bretton Woods system of which global capital flows
were strongly constrained, financial crisis rarely occurred. But since the 1980s, the time point
that current financialization began, the financial crisis again became a frequent, clinical event
in the world economy.

Figure 3

The data clearly shows a transition and its repercussion from a Keynesian-guide Bretton
Wood economic system to the neoliberal one, as explained by Magdoff and Paul Sweezy
(1987), the expansion of the financial sector and peculiarly debt as a solution to the stagnation
that ended the United State’s post WWII economic boom has turned America into a ‘casino
society’.
Despite the fierce critics, scholars also discovered a strong positive relationship between
financial development and economic development. By a sufficient number of empirical
studies at both micro and macro levels, Levine (2005) suggests that there is a strong positive
link between the functioning of the financial system and long-run economic growth. Both
financial intermediaries and markets matter for growth, A well-functioned financial system
reduces external financial constraints facing firms, but current theory and evidence are not
able to show that financialization is merely an appendage or derivate of economic growth.
Later this positive correlation was evaluated by Beck, Degryseb and Kneer (2014), who
discovered a U-shaped relationship that shows the benefit of financial development on
economic growth only lasts to a certain point, since then the power of the financial sector
acts adversely to economic growth. They concluded that: “In the long run financial
intermediation increases growth and reduces growth volatility. […]Critically, financial system
size, especially non-intermediation service, has a positive relationship with volatility in highincome countries over the medium-term.” (Beck, Degryseb, and Kneer 2014:55) In addition
to their argument, Cecchetti and Kharroubi (2012) focused on studying the relationship
between financial development and economic growth in the medium run among advanced
economies. Their data shows the U-shape pattern proposed by Beck, Degryseb and Kneer,
and discovered that the intermediation financial services are fundamentally detrimental to
productivity growth. Figure 4 shows the average private credit to GDP ratio on the x-axis and
average GDP per worker growth on the y-axis, the regression line shows that there is a
negative correlation between the two variables after the proportion of average private credit
to GDP exceeds 0.3 while the positive correlation between the two variables before that point
remains modest. The U-shape is more clearly shown in Figure 5 that the financial
intermediation services contribute positively to average GDP per-worker growth within the
range of -0.003 to 0.001. And figure 6 shows that there is a linear negative correlation
between the growth of average financial intermediation employment and average real GDPper-worker growth.

Conclusion

Financialization is an influential, ongoing process that has branches across various disciplines.
This essay has first introduced the methodology to assess the conceptualizations given by
different scholars, when conceptualizing, thinkers should first be explicit about their level of
analysis, and then be careful about the risks of conceptual dilution and solidification. Based
on such methodology, I assessed three popular definitions given by Gerald Epstein, Greta
Krippner and Engelbert Stockhammer, they are three distinctive approaches for us to
recognize financialization at a macro and meso level. Due to space limitations, the
conceptualization of financialization on the micro level is not mentioned. After that, this essay
looked into the causes of financialization in the post-80s context, which can be concluded as:
“ Initiated by structural change started in the United States and facilitated by technological
advancement and changing social culture.” In the last part of this essay, I assessed the effects
of financialization with empirical evidence and hard data. Financialization has an adverse
impact on medium-run economic growth, injects volatility into society and exacerbates
inequality with rich counties and individuals being even wealthier and the poor getting worse.
However, there is also a strong positive correlation between financial development and longrun economic growth. Given that the causality between the correlation is yet clear, it is
possible that these repercussions of financialization could be primarily caused by other
external factors and financialization just works as a catalyst.
